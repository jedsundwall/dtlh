# 2026-05-09 — Form the Trust as a Washington nonprofit corporation

**Status:** PROPOSED
**Affects:** [`current/02-entity-structure.md`](../current/02-entity-structure.md)

## Context

dtlh.org launched 2026-05-04. Within five days, neighbors pledged $1.4M of non-binding indications of interest. One prospective anchor wrote in saying:

> We would be interested in potentially invest, but would need to get to a point where we are having clearer indications of a legal structure before saying anything specific.

The pledges are not money — they are indications of interest tracked in email and a spreadsheet. There is no entity to receive them, hold predevelopment funds, or sign anything with the seller. The signal from the anchor email is that further engagement requires a credible counterparty: not necessarily a fully-papered investment vehicle, but a real entity that exists, has a name, has a bank account, and demonstrates fiduciary discipline.

We have probono legal support available for entity formation. We do not yet have a securities counsel relationship; that is the next critical hire and is separate from the corporate / nonprofit lawyer who will handle the formation.

The seller is a church. We are exploring whether the church will offer seller financing. This is relevant because, if the church carries paper for ~80% of the purchase price, the equity we need to raise for acquisition drops from ~$5M to ~$1M, which fits comfortably into a single Reg D 506(c) round of accredited anchors — making the case for moving on entity formation now, rather than waiting for a fully papered offering, even stronger.

## Decision

We will incorporate **Downtown Loyal Heights Trust, Inc.** (or a comparable name; final name to be confirmed by counsel for word-restriction compliance) as a Washington nonprofit corporation under [RCW 24.03A](https://app.leg.wa.gov/RCW/default.aspx?cite=24.03A) within the next 7–10 business days.

Specifications:

- Member-managed nonprofit, with membership eligibility limited to Loyal Heights residents (boundary defined in bylaws).
- Three initial directors. Not the long-term board — these are people willing to serve as fiduciaries during formation and resign or rotate as the long-term board takes shape.
- Purpose clause drafted to support both 501(c)(3) and 501(c)(4) eligibility.
- Standard IRS-required dissolution clause directing residual assets to another 501(c)(3) or to a federal/state/local government for a public purpose.
- Registered agent at a Washington street address.

The Trust will:

- Be the public face of the project.
- Pursue 501(c)(3) recognition via Form 1023, with a fiscal sponsor used in the interim for accepting tax-deductible predevelopment donations.
- Hold the future manager interest in DTLH Holdings, LLC, when that LLC is formed (separate decision, expected 30–60 days later).
- Reserve "major decisions" (sale, change of mission, dissolution) regardless of LLC cap-table.

The Trust will **not**:

- Receive investor capital.
- Solicit or accept funds for the LLC's eventual securities offerings.

Investment capital will flow only into the LLC, only under offering documents drafted by securities counsel.

## Consequences

Once the Articles are filed:

- Director fiduciary duties begin immediately. Initial directors should be aware of this and will get a short orientation.
- The Trust must file an annual report with WA SOS (~$20/year) or face administrative dissolution.
- Form 1023 must be filed within 27 months of formation for retroactive 501(c)(3) recognition.
- Form 990-N (postcard) due annually while gross receipts are under $50K.
- The site's "Where This Is" section needs an update reflecting that the Trust now exists. (Draft language is in the working memo.)
- Predevelopment donations (legal, environmental, design) can be solicited via fiscal sponsor while 501(c)(3) is pending.
- Anchor outreach can now reference an actual entity, addressing the "clearer legal structure" concern.

We are explicitly **not** deciding the following with this record. They are deferred to subsequent decisions:

- Final legal name (counsel confirms).
- Bylaws specifics (drafted next; will become its own decision record).
- Long-term board composition.
- LLC formation timing and operating-agreement structure.
- 501(c)(3) vs. 501(c)(4) primary classification.

## Alternatives considered

**Do nothing yet, wait for a real deal first.** Rejected. The anchor signal is that engagement requires structure. Waiting risks anchors firming commitments to other vehicles or drifting away. Forming now costs ~$200 over two years even if the project dies (filing fee + annual reports + dissolution); losing anchor momentum costs the project.

**Form a Washington cooperative association first.** Rejected. Cooperative securities exemptions in Washington are narrower than in CA, MN, and VT (the states where NEIC, EBPREC, and VREC formed), and Washington has no equivalent of California's AB 816. The nonprofit + LLC pattern delivers the same governance with stronger securities footing.

**Form just an LLC, no nonprofit.** Rejected. Without the nonprofit governance shell there is no mission lock and no fiduciary container for the long-term community-ownership claim. Anchors negotiating directly against an LLC structure end up with disproportionate leverage; the Trust exists to balance that.

**Use a fiscal sponsor as the only entity for now, defer formation.** Rejected as a sole path. Fiscal sponsorship is appropriate for receiving predevelopment donations while 501(c)(3) is pending, but the anchor needs to see "an entity exists," not "we're a project under another organization." Fiscal sponsorship will be used alongside the Trust formation, not instead of it.
