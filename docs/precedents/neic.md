# NorthEast Investment Cooperative (NEIC)

**Where:** Northeast Minneapolis, Minnesota
**Founded:** November 2011
**Legal form:** Cooperative association under Minnesota Statutes Chapter 308A
**Why we care:** The closest precedent in spirit to what we're trying to do. Often described as the first U.S. commercial-property investment cooperative.

## Brief

39 founding member-owners incorporated NEIC at the end of 2011. Members buy a $1,000 Class A membership share — one share per member, one vote per member, regardless of how many shares any individual owns. The cooperative pooled roughly $80,000 from members for its first acquisition (in tandem with a member tenant who bought the adjacent building) and closed on 2506 Central Avenue NE in summer 2013. By the end of 2012 NEIC had grown to about 90 members; over time it has acquired and rehabbed multiple buildings on Central Avenue.

The capital structure is the part most directly portable to our model. NEIC's Articles of Incorporation authorize up to $7.75M of total capital stock across:

- **Class A common** — $10 par, voting, residency-restricted (Minnesota residents). One member, one vote.
- **Class B nonvoting common** — $10 par, no dividends.
- **Class C preferred** — $5,000 par, sold to Class A members for expansion capital.
- **Class D preferred** — $500 par, similar role at a smaller dollar amount.

Class C and Class D were sold under the Minnesota cooperative securities exemption (now codified around Minn. Stat. § 80A.46 and Rule 2876.5012), restricted to Minnesota residents who were already Class A members. The board was elected by Class A members.

## What we're borrowing

- **Share-class architecture.** A residency-restricted voting class plus non-voting preferred classes for outside capital is exactly the design we want at the LLC level — re-mapped to Class V (voting), Class A (anchor), and Class N (neighbor-economic) respectively. NEIC's Articles bake the residency restriction into the entity's organic documents rather than relying on bylaws alone, which is the right move; bylaws are easier to change than Articles.
- **Sequence: incorporate first, raise second.** NEIC formed the entity before raising any meaningful capital. Once the entity existed it could open a bank account, sign membership agreements, and accept member shares. We are following the same sequence — file the Trust first, then form the LLC and offer interests once securities counsel has the offering papered.
- **Public Articles.** NEIC publishes its Articles, which makes them a free template for anyone copying the model. We should plan to do the same for ours once filed — both Articles and bylaws as public PDFs in this repo.

## What's different in our case

- **Entity form.** We are forming a nonprofit + LLC, not a single cooperative, because Washington's cooperative securities exemptions are narrower than Minnesota's. Washington has no AB 816 equivalent.
- **Scale.** NEIC's first deal was ~$80K of member equity for a building purchased alongside a member tenant. Our acquisition target is in the millions, with a total project cost potentially in the tens of millions. That changes the role of accredited anchors and brings construction debt into play in a way NEIC has not faced.
- **Anchor structure.** NEIC's small-share, uniform-price model doesn't fit a Reg D 506(c) raise from accredited investors at $1M each. We need a separate Class A (Anchor) tranche with preferred return and observer rights — something NEIC doesn't have a direct equivalent of.
