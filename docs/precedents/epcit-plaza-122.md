# East Portland Community Investment Trust (Plaza 122)

**Where:** Portland, OR (122nd & Market)
**Founded:** 2017
**Legal form:** Oregon C corporation (with the option to elect private REIT status); sponsored and incubated by Mercy Corps Northwest
**Why we care:** The cleanest *financial* precedent. The EPCIT capital stack is the canonical Phase 1 / Phase 2 acquisition structure for community real estate, and the bank-issued Letter of Credit is the technical move that makes investor downside loss-protected.

## Brief

EPCIT acquired Plaza 122 — a 16,000 sq ft strip mall at the corner of 122nd Avenue and Market Street in East Portland — in 2017 for **$1.2M**. The capital stack:

| Source | Amount | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Mercy Corps Northwest (sponsoring nonprofit) | $220,000 | Initial sponsor equity |
| Two impact investors (subordinated debt) | $230,000 | Sub-debt — bridges sponsor equity and senior loan |
| Beneficial State Bank (later Northwest Bank) | $900,000 | First-lien mortgage |
| **Total acquisition** | **$1,200,000** | |

The community investors did **not** put up the down payment. Instead, the $450K of "initial equity" provided by Mercy Corps and impact investors becomes the equity that residents *buy back* over time — through $10–$100/month auto-debit purchases of shares in the corporation.

A bank-issued **Letter of Credit** backstops investor downside, so resident shares are effectively loss-protected: a resident who sells their shares can withdraw at original cost. The financial-action course "Moving from Owing to Owning" is required before purchase, and eligibility is restricted to residents of four specific zip codes around the property.

By 2022, 328 households had invested. Share price grew from $10 in 2017 to $19.02 by 2022, with a 7.9% average dividend.

Mercy Corps NW later spun out **CIT Services** as a national licensing arm to replicate the structure in other cities, funded in part by a $1.75M JPMorgan Chase grant.

## What we're borrowing

- **The capital stack as a template.** Sponsor equity + impact-investor sub-debt + senior bank debt = acquisition. Residents buy in *after* acquisition, gradually, via auto-debit. Our anchor investors slot directly into the sub-debt position, scaled up. This solves the chicken-and-egg problem of needing to acquire before community equity can be raised under a securities offering.
- **Bank-issued Letter of Credit for community equity loss protection.** This is the magic that makes Phase 2 community equity legitimately low-risk, and it's the kind of thing you have to negotiate with the senior lender at acquisition time. Open the conversation early with whichever community bank or CDFI is willing to underwrite (Beneficial State Bank, Craft3, Verity Credit Union are obvious calls).
- **Geographic eligibility tied to zip codes.** Clean, defensible, easy to verify. We can use the same approach with Loyal Heights' boundary.
- **Pre-purchase financial education.** The "Moving from Owing to Owning" course is both legitimately useful and a smart legal/comms move — it makes investor sophistication a documented part of the structure rather than a securities-law liability.
- **Dividend + appreciation, not just appreciation.** EPCIT's 7.9% dividend is the model for a community equity instrument that pays during the hold, not only at exit.

## What's different in our case

- **C-corp with REIT option vs. nonprofit + LLC.** EPCIT's Oregon C-corp structure (with the option to elect private REIT status) is heavier than what we need at acquisition. We can defer the REIT decision until Phase 2 and, if it's the right move, possibly convert the LLC to a corporation at that point. Worth a tax-counsel conversation at the right time, not now.
- **Sponsor equity source.** EPCIT's $220K came from Mercy Corps NW's own balance sheet. We don't have that. Our equivalent is anchor accredited investors writing $1M checks; the Trust itself isn't the source of risk capital.
- **Scale.** Plaza 122 is a $1.2M acquisition. Our acquisition is $5M (with seller financing potentially reducing the equity required) and total project cost potentially $50M. The capital stack works mechanically at that scale but the senior lender becomes a more demanding party.
- **Investor education requirement.** Worth considering whether to require something analogous to "Moving from Owing to Owning" for our community equity investors. Probably yes, both for substantive reasons and to mirror the EPCIT pattern that regulators are familiar with.
