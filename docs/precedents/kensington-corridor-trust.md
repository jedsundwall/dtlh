# Kensington Corridor Trust (KCT)

**Where:** Philadelphia, PA (Kensington corridor, ~33-property portfolio)
**Founded:** September 2019
**Legal form:** Two tethered entities — a Pennsylvania perpetual purpose trust + a 501(c)(3) nonprofit
**Why we care:** The closest *governance* precedent to our sketch. The nine-seat Trust Stewardship Committee composition formula is directly copyable, and the "trust enforcer" concept is worth replicating even where Washington doesn't legally require it.

## Brief

KCT was founded in September 2019 by four organizations: Impact Services CDC, Philadelphia Industrial Development Corporation (PIDC), Shift Capital, and IF lab. The structure pairs a **Pennsylvania perpetual purpose trust (PPT)** that holds the real estate with a **501(c)(3) nonprofit** that handles charitable and programmatic activity. The two are legally tethered: the trust holds the assets and the nonprofit operates programs, with overlapping but not identical governance.

The trust is governed by a **nine-seat Trust Stewardship Committee** with a fixed composition formula:

- 2 Kensington residents
- 2 Kensington small-business owners
- 2 community leaders
- 2 youth leaders (ages 16–21)
- 1 crossover seat from the nonprofit board

PPT structure under Pennsylvania law requires an external **trust enforcer** with no financial ties to the trust — a person legally responsible for ensuring the trust pursues its stated purpose. Pennsylvania makes this mandatory; we'd consider it a feature even where Washington law doesn't require it.

By 2024, KCT held about 33 properties along the Kensington corridor.

## What we're borrowing

- **Nine-seat composition formula.** The split between residents, business owners, community leaders, and youth is a defensible, documented governance pattern. Worth proposing as the model for our Trust's board structure (though we'd map "Kensington" → "Loyal Heights" and decide whether to keep small-business-owner seats given our specific corner).
- **Youth seats (16–21).** This is the kind of bake-it-in-now governance choice that's hard to add later. It signals intergenerational stewardship and brings perspectives that older boards systematically miss.
- **Trust enforcer.** Even in Washington, where we'd structure as a nonprofit corporation rather than a PPT, designating an external party with no financial ties whose job is to push back when the Trust drifts from its purpose is good governance hygiene. We can build it in via bylaws as an "ombudsperson" or "purpose monitor" role even without statutory backing.
- **Two tethered entities.** Maps onto our Trust + LLC sketch, though our split is governance/asset-holding rather than governance/programmatic. The principle of separating mission lock from operating activity carries.

## What's different in our case

- **Perpetual purpose trust vs. nonprofit corporation.** Pennsylvania has explicit PPT-enabling statutes. Washington does not. We are using a nonprofit corporation in the governance role rather than a PPT, which means we don't have the same statutory mission-lock mechanism. We compensate with bylaws-level supermajority requirements for changes to mission, sale, or dissolution.
- **Asset scope.** KCT operates a portfolio of ~33 properties. We're starting with one corner of one block. Their governance is built for ongoing portfolio decisions; ours initially is built around one major project decision.
- **Programmatic vs. investment.** KCT's nonprofit handles charitable and programmatic activity. Our nonprofit's role is primarily governance, not programs (at least initially). We may add programmatic activity later — community space management, retail tenant mix support — but it's not on the critical path.
