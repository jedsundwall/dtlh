# NYC Real Estate Investment Cooperative (NYC REIC)

**Where:** New York, NY
**Founded:** 2015
**Legal form:** New York not-for-profit corporation (with planned per-property special-purpose-entity LLCs)
**Why we care:** The closest *organizational* precedent. They ran the play we're running — pre-formation pledges, public launch event, fiscal sponsorship while waiting for regulators — and the two-tier "nonprofit corp + per-asset LLC" structure they landed on is the model we're adapting.

## Brief

NYC REIC was incubated by 596 Acres (founded by attorney Paula Z. Segal). In March 2015 a public survey produced **$1.2M+ in non-binding pledges**. The public launch event on April 28, 2015 drew 350+ attendees; 200+ joined that day. The first member meeting was in May 2015 with about $4,000 banked at the Brooklyn Cooperative Federal Credit Union.

Then came the regulatory wait. The New York Attorney General's Real Estate Finance Bureau requires approval before a NY nonprofit can accept member money for real estate investment. NYC REIC didn't get that approval until **December 2017** — about two and a half years after launch. During that window the organization operated under fiscal sponsorship by **Flux Factory**, a 501(c)(3) arts incubator. Members paid dues but real-estate-purpose investment had to wait until the AG cleared them.

The Steering Committee chose the legal structure as: a **member-controlled not-for-profit corporation with special-purpose-entity LLC subsidiaries** for each property they would eventually acquire. Member governance at the nonprofit; per-property liability and economic structure at the LLC.

## What we're borrowing

- **Pre-formation pledges as proof of seriousness.** NYC REIC turned a survey of $1.2M into a 350-person launch event into 200 same-day signups. We are at $1.4M in pledges after one week. The play of formalizing those pledges into Indications of Interest, then converting once the entity exists, is well-trodden.
- **The two-tier structure.** Member-elected nonprofit corporation as the governance layer; per-property LLC as the asset/investment-vehicle layer. Maps directly onto our Trust + LLC sketch.
- **Fiscal sponsorship as a bridge.** While waiting for IRS and state regulators, sponsor under an existing 501(c)(3) so donations can be tax-deductible. NYC REIC used Flux Factory; we'll use a Seattle equivalent (Sustainable Seattle, SEED, 501 Commons).
- **Launch event as a forcing function.** The April 28, 2015 event converted attention into membership. Worth replicating once the Trust is filed and we have something concrete to invite people into.

## What's different in our case

- **No NY-AG-style state regulator gate.** Washington doesn't have a parallel pre-approval requirement for a nonprofit accepting investment money. Our gating constraint is securities counsel + IRS, not a state attorney general.
- **Member-controlled vs. board-controlled.** NYC REIC's bylaws reflect a particular vision of member democracy that we may or may not want to copy wholesale. Worth reading their structure carefully against the homepage promise of resident voting before locking in bylaws.
- **Scale.** NYC REIC's individual property acquisitions are smaller than what we're contemplating. We need a structure that can absorb $5M anchor equity in Phase 1 plus $30–40M of construction debt + LP equity in Phase 2 — a heavier institutional layer than what NYC REIC was designed for.
